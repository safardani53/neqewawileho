Ashes 2005 hero Marcus Trescothick, 43, to retire from cricket at the end of the season after 27-yea

Publish date: 2024-07-07

MARCUS TRESCOTHICK has announced he will retire from all cricket at the end of the season after a glittering 27-year career.

The veteran opening batsman, 43, played 76 Tests for England including all five in the memorable 2005 Ashes series.

One of only two players from that famous summer still playing - along with Ian Bell - "Banger" will call time on his Somerset career in September.

On his decision, Trescothick revealed: "It's been an incredible 27 years and I've loved every minute of it.

"However, everything has to come to an end eventually. I’ve been discussing my future with the club and my family for a while.

"We felt that now was the appropriate time to make this announcement in order for both the club and I to put plans in place.

"This club, the members and the supporters mean so much to me.

"There are so many memories that I will cherish forever, and the club will always hold a truly special place in my heart."

Trescothick on the origin of his Banger nickname

Trescothick has played nearly 1,000 professional matches, scoring 120 centuries in that run, since his 1993 debut as a fresh-faced 17-year-old.

That year, the batsman - famed for his stand-and-deliver, limited-footwork style - was given his nickname of Banger.

Tres explained: "My diet was sausages then, in no particular order, sausages, chips, sausages, toast, sausages, beans, sausages, cheese, sausages, eggs, and the occasional sausage."

But the endearing name held more meaning in his later years, after his destructive approach to batting helped launch a new era of English cricket.

After making his international debut in 2000, Trescothick became an integral part of his national side in both Test and ODI formats.

All in all, the Somerset man plundered 5,825 runs at a superb average  of 43.79 - with a top score of 219 - while notching over 4,000 one-day runs at nearly 40.

COMING BACK TO ME

Unquestionably the highlight of his illustrious career, though, came in the famous summer of 2005.

Going into the home Ashes series as heavy underdogs against Australia, Trescothick formed a blistering opening partnership with Andrew Strauss.

After losing the First Test at Lord's, the openers set the tone for the rest of the series with a barrage of aggressive stands, taking the attack to Glenn McGrath, Brett Lee, Jason Gillespie and Shane Warne.

Trescothick hit a series-high 64 fours that series as he ended up as second-top run-scorer, with a whopping 431 runs.

But disaster struck months later, while on tour to India in 2006.

In February that year, the Somerset slugger left the tour abruptly, citing personal reasons - later called a virus.

However, it turned out he was suffering from clinical depression, a topic the batsman talk about openly and honestly in his fantastic autobiography, Coming Back To Me.

Trescothick would go on to play against Pakistan that summer, but never played for his country again, ending his career far too early.

MOST READ IN CRICKET

But the hard-hitting opener has continued to play on the county circuit, an ever-present for Somerset, now in his 27th and final season at Taunton.

Tres has scored over 26,000 first-class runs for Somerset, as well as 12,229 List-A runs, while chipping in with 93 wickets with his part-time medium pace.

The famous slip-catcher also held 95 Test catches, 49 in ODIs, 560 in the four-day game and 149 county one-dayers.
